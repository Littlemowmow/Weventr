# SideQuest Landing Page — Copy Integration Plan
## Mapping Market Psychology to Every Section

This document maps the Gen Z group travel research directly onto the current landing page. For each section, I show what's there now, what the research says, and the exact changes to make.

## SECTION 1: HERO

### What's there now
- Badge: "Beta Launching Soon"
- Headline: "The World Is Waiting. Your groupchat is frozen."
- Subheadline: "SideQuest is the app that turns 'I'm down if you are' into an actual trip — with group voting, budget alignment, and hidden gems your guidebook doesn't know about. Built by college students, for college students."
- CTAs: "Claim Your Spot →" / "Try the demo"
- Floating cards: Barcelona vote result, $85/day, 5 going, 80% agreed

### What the research says
- "Never makes it out of the group chat" is the single most resonant phrase in this cultural space (36M+ TikTok posts)
- The planner friend is your primary user — speak to them directly
- "I'm down if you are" is already recognized sarcastic code for "will do zero actual planning" — you're using this well
- "Built by college students, for college students" limits future positioning and the audit flagged this too
- Gen Z responds to specificity over abstraction — "The World Is Waiting" is aspirational but vague

### Changes

**Headline options (pick one):**

Option A (most direct, leverages the #1 phrase):
> "The trip that actually makes it out of the group chat."

Option B (confrontational, high scroll-stop energy):  
> "Your group chat has planned 47 trips. You've been on zero."

Option C (keep the current structure but sharpen it):
> "The world is waiting. Your group chat is not going to figure it out."

**Why:** "Frozen" is good but passive. The research shows people describe their group chats as places where trips actively *die* — not freeze. The language is about death, not stasis. "Die in the group chat," "never makes it out," "the trip is dead" — this is the vocabulary.

**Subheadline rewrite:**
> "SideQuest turns 'I'm down if you are' into an actual trip. Group voting so nobody gets ignored, budget alignment so nobody goes quiet, and hidden gems your guidebook doesn't know about."

**Why:** Break it into the three pain points the research surfaced as most emotionally charged: (1) decision paralysis / nobody commits, (2) budget kills the vibe silently, (3) tourist trap anxiety. Remove "Built by college students, for college students" — it limits you and the founder story is better told in FAQ.

**Floating cards — keep but adjust copy:**
- Change "Vote Result" → "The group has spoken" (more casual, mirrors how they talk)
- Keep "$85/day" — budget transparency before commitment is the #1 thing users asked for
- Change "80% agreed" → "4 out of 5 are in" (more human, less dashboard)

## SECTION 2: SOCIAL PROOF TICKER

### What's there now
Scrolling pills: 🌴 Girls Trip to Tulum, 🌴 Spring Break in Miami, ❄️ Ski Week in the Alps, etc.

### What the research says
- This is aspirational, not real social proof — sophisticated visitors will clock this
- But the *trip type labels* are culturally loaded and useful — "Euro Summer 2026," "Girls Trip," "Study Abroad" are identity phrases Gen Z uses
- The research surfaced "micro-cation" (short 3-4 day trips) as a Gen Z preference — currently missing
- "Destination dupes" (lesser-known alternatives to crowded spots) had 400K+ social media posts — this is a trend you could signal

### Changes

**Reframe the ticker from fake social proof to cultural signal.** Instead of implying these are real trips being planned, position them as trip *types* people search for:

Replace current pills with a mix that reflects actual Gen Z trip vocabulary:
> 🌴 Spring Break · 🇪🇺 Euro Summer · 🍜 Tokyo Food Tour · 👯‍♀️ Girls Trip · ⛷️ Ski Week · 🏝️ Island Hopping · 🎓 Study Abroad Weekend · 🚗 Road Trip · 🏔️ 3-Day Micro-Trip · 💎 Destination Dupe · 🏄‍♂️ Surf Trip · 🎭 City Break

**Why:** Adding "Study Abroad Weekend," "3-Day Micro-Trip," and "Destination Dupe" signals that you understand *how* they actually travel (short bursts, budget-conscious, anti-mainstream). These are search terms and identity markers, not just destinations.

**Optional enhancement:** If the API waitlist count is real and growing, prefix the ticker with a subtle line: "Trips being planned right now →" — but only if the count is real. No fake numbers.

## SECTION 3: PROBLEM / DEMO

### What's there now
- Headline: "Don't let the trip die in the Group Chat."
- Subheadline: "Sound familiar? See how SideQuest fixes it."
- 4 floating problem cards: Budget Fight, Date Clash, Indecision, Logistics Fail
- Animated group chat with Sarah, Mike, Jess, You
- Chat ends with "..." then SideQuest prompt

### What the research says
- This is the strongest section on the page — the animated chat is genuinely original
- The four problem cards map perfectly to the top pain points from research
- The exact phrases people use: "let me check with work" (beginning of the end), "I picked last time!", "I thought [name] was doing it" — you're already close
- The "..." silence beat is emotionally accurate — the research shows the death of a trip is always *silence*, never an official cancellation
- The five-stage emotional arc (Spark → Wall → Silence → Death → Meme) is the most recognized pattern

### Changes

**Headline — keep it.** "Don't let the trip die in the Group Chat" uses the exact language. This is your best headline on the page. (The audit noted your footer tagline "So the trip doesn't die in the groupchat" is the strongest line — this section's headline is the other one. Lean into this framing everywhere.)

**Problem cards — sharpen the copy to use exact verbatim phrases from research:**

Budget Fight (current):
> "The boat rental is $800?? 🛥️" / "That puts us way over budget" / "Let's find something cheaper"

Budget Fight (revised — use the visceral specificity the research showed):
> "Wait… I thought we were splitting this?" / "Bro the Airbnb is $200/NIGHT??" / "Nobody told me about the boat 💀"

**Why:** The research showed the most emotionally charged budget moments are *surprise costs* and *splitting confusion*, not abstract "over budget" discussions. The $30 seafood dinner that ended a friendship. The three-euro Venmo request. The moments when someone realizes the group assumed a different budget. Make it about the shock, not the math.

Date Clash (current):
> "How about June 12-15?" / "Can't. Cousin's wedding." / "July 4th weekend?" / "Flights are insane then 😭"

Date Clash (revised):
> "June works for me!" / "Let me check with work" / "..." / "...so is this still happening?"

**Why:** "Let me check with work" is THE phrase the research identified as the beginning of the end. Everyone recognizes it. And "so is this still happening?" is the desperate last message before a trip officially dies. This sequence tells the whole five-stage arc in four messages.

Indecision (current):
> "So where are we going?" / "Idk you pick 🤷‍♂️" / "I picked last time!"

Indecision (revised):
> "I'm down for whatever" / "Same, you guys decide" / "..." / "So no one's deciding??"

**Why:** "I'm down for whatever" is sarcastic code for "will contribute nothing" — the research showed this phrase is universally mocked. "Same, you guys decide" is the exact echo that kills momentum. The silence followed by the exasperated question mirrors the real pattern.

Logistics Fail (keep mostly as-is — it's already strong):
> "Did anyone book the train?" / "..." / "I thought Mike was doing it 💀"

This already uses the exact research pattern: assumption → silence → blame. The skull emoji is perfect Gen Z punctuation. Keep it.

**Animated chat — minor tweaks:**

Change Sarah's message from "guys we NEED to plan this Barcelona trip" to:
> "ok so are we actually doing Barcelona or not"

**Why:** The research shows the *re-ask* is more emotionally charged than the initial pitch. The trip has already been discussed and stalled. Sarah isn't proposing — she's frustrated and trying to revive a dead conversation. This is the Stage 3 moment (silence → desperate check-in).

Change the final "..." to hold for a beat longer before the SideQuest prompt appears. The silence IS the emotional climax. Let it breathe.

## SECTION 4: FEATURES

### What's there now
- Headline: "From chaos to consensus"
- Subheadline: "Three tools that replace 200 unread messages."
- Feature 1: Swipe & Vote
- Feature 2: Budget Lock
- Feature 3: Squad Itinerary

### What the research says
- "200 unread messages" is already a phrase that appears verbatim in the research — great instinct
- The #1 feature request was commitment mechanisms with deadlines (not currently shown)
- The #2 request was democratic decision-making (you have this — Swipe & Vote)
- The #3 was budget transparency before committing (you have this — Budget Lock)
- The #4 was centralized hub replacing 4-7 apps (partially addressed)
- The #5 was distribution of planning labor (you have this — Squad Itinerary)
- Feature names feel slightly over-branded ("Squad Itinerary") — the research shows people describe these needs in plain language

### Changes

**Headline — keep.** "From chaos to consensus" is clean and accurate.

**Subheadline — already strong but could be even more specific:**
> "Three features that replace the Google Doc nobody opens, the poll with zero overlap, and the friend doing all the work."

**Why:** The research surfaced these three objects (the abandoned Google Doc, the useless Doodle poll, the exhausted planner friend) as universally recognized symbols of group trip failure. Naming them directly triggers instant recognition.

**Feature 1: Swipe & Vote — adjust body copy:**

Current: "No more 'I'm down if you are.' Everyone swipes on destinations and activities independently. SideQuest finds what your group actually agrees on — no awkward standoffs."

Revised:
> "Everyone swipes on destinations and activities on their own — no awkward standoffs, no 'I'm down for whatever' from the friend who never decides. SideQuest surfaces what your group actually agrees on."

**Why:** Swap "I'm down if you are" (used in hero) for "I'm down for whatever" (different phrase, same energy, avoids repetition). "The friend who never decides" names the archetype directly — the research showed people instantly picture someone specific when they hear this.

**Feature 2: Budget Lock — adjust body copy:**

Current: "Set a group budget everyone's comfortable with before anyone books anything. Real-time expense tracking and automatic splits so nobody's chasing Venmo requests three months later."

Revised:
> "Everyone sees real costs before anyone commits — no sticker shock, no one going quiet when prices come up. Set a budget your whole group is comfortable with, and automatic splits mean nobody's chasing passive-aggressive Venmo requests three months later."

**Why:** "Going quiet when prices come up" is exactly how the research describes the budget ghost — the friend who disappears when money gets real. "Passive-aggressive Venmo requests" is a phrase with deep cultural resonance (the research identified it as cultural shorthand for post-trip money awkwardness). "Sticker shock" names the exact moment trips die.

**Feature 3: Squad Itinerary — adjust body copy:**

Current: "Build a shared itinerary everyone can see and edit. Drag, drop, remix. No more one person doing all the work while everyone else says 'whatever you think.'"

Revised:
> "One shared plan everyone can see, edit, and actually contribute to. No more one person sending 47 unanswered messages while everyone else is just 'vibing.' If you added it, your name's on it."

**Why:** "47 unanswered messages" is more specific and painful than "doing all the work." "Vibing" is the exact Gen Z term for contributing nothing while appearing chill. "Your name's on it" addresses the accountability gap — the research showed planner burnout comes from invisible labor.

**Bottom link — rewrite:**

Current: "See all features — Trip Randomizer, Budget Settlement, Gamification & more →"

Revised:
> "Plus: deadline locks so nobody flakes, a trip randomizer for when you're stuck, and a few surprises →"

**Why:** "Deadline locks so nobody flakes" directly addresses the #1 feature request from the research (commitment mechanisms). "Flakes" is the exact word they use. "Gamification" means nothing to a consumer — hide it behind the experience.

### What the research says
- The #1 hesitation is "why try again" (flake fatigue — they've been burned by failed planning before)

### Changes

**Q1 (keep, it's strong):** "Why SideQuest? What makes it different?" — current answer is solid.

**Q2 (rewrite to address flake fatigue directly):**

Q: "What if my friends always flake?"
A: "That's literally why we built this. SideQuest has voting deadlines, budget locks, and shared itineraries that make it harder for anyone to quietly back out. When everyone can see who's voted, who's committed, and what it costs — the 'let me check with work' excuse loses its power. We can't fix your flaky friend, but we can make it really obvious when they're flaking."

**Why:** This replaces the current Q2 ("Isn't this just another travel booking app?") which is important but less emotionally resonant. Move the booking clarification into Q1's answer instead. The flake question is the #1 emotional objection — they've been hurt before and need to believe this time will be different.

**Q3 (keep, sharpen slightly):**

Q: "Why not just use a spreadsheet or group chat?"
A: Keep current answer but add at the end: "Plus, be honest — when's the last time everyone actually opened the Google Doc?"

**Q4 (rewrite — AI is currently introduced out of nowhere):**

Either remove Q4 entirely (AI isn't shown in any feature section, so it feels disconnected), OR rewrite:

Q: "Is there AI involved?"

**Q5 (keep as-is):** "Does everyone in the group need the app?" — solid answer.

Q: "Is SideQuest free?"

**Why:** "Exclusive founding pricing" is vague and creates anxiety. A relatable anchor ("less than one overpriced tourist trap coffee") gives a ballpark without committing to a number and uses humor that fits the voice.

**Q7 (keep as-is):** "Who's building SideQuest?" — the Michigan student story is authentic and effective.

**Q8 (keep as-is):** "When is the beta launching?" — fine for now.

**Add Q9:**

Q: "Why not just go solo?"
A: "Solo travel is amazing — seriously. But if you've got a group of friends you actually want to travel with, the problem isn't the people. It's that group chats are terrible planning tools. SideQuest doesn't replace solo travel — it makes group travel actually possible so you don't have to choose."

**Why:** 75% of Gen Z planned a solo trip recently. The "I'll just go solo" escape is the biggest competitor to your product — not another app, but giving up on the group entirely. Acknowledge it, validate it, then redirect.

## GLOBAL VOICE RULES

Based on the research, these rules should apply across every piece of copy on the page:

1. **Use "gc" and "group chat" interchangeably.** Your audience says both.
2. **Write like you're texting, not marketing.** Short sentences. Fragments are fine. Emojis are punctuation, not decoration.
3. **Name the archetypes.** "The friend who..." is more powerful than abstract descriptions of problems.
4. **Use their exact phrases.** "I'm down for whatever." "Let me check with work." "I thought [name] was doing it." These are cultural passwords.
5. **The tone is sarcastic-but-hopeful.** They expect trips to fail. You're the reason this one won't. Don't be earnest — be knowing.
6. **Never say "streamline," "consolidate," "all-in-one," "seamless," or "leverage."** These are B2B words. Your audience says "just works," "actually happens," "doesn't suck."
7. **Specificity > abstraction.** "$200/night Airbnb" hits harder than "budget conflicts." "47 unanswered messages" hits harder than "communication challenges."
8. **The group chat is the villain.** Not your friends. Not travel. The group chat is where good ideas go to die. SideQuest is the exit.
